From a 650 ml bottle (bottled June 2015) into a tulip glass. Reviewed from notes dated May 9, 2015.

A-The beer is an orange-tinted hay color. Pretty light but quite murky. The head is white and starts out at two fingers tall before fading slowly to a film. The foam leaves chunks of lace on the glass.

S-The aroma is full of brett notes and dry-hopped tropical fruitiness. I sniff some peach, pineapple, white grape, and orange. There is a good hard pull on my glands from the sourness, but the beer is not overly acidic or puckering. A touch of medicinal bitterness creeps in as the beer warms.

T-The taste is less interesting than the smell, but not bad at all. On the palate, the beer provides more grain taste and a dry, earthy, bretty character. I taste some grapefruit, a bit of white grape tartness, and very vague tropical notes. A light bitterness builds with each sip and the finish is pretty dusty.

M-The body is medium in heft with a softer carbonation than normal for the style.

O-I enjoyed this beer, and in particular, I liked the smell quite a bit. It lacks that huge acidity than so many of Avery's beers seem to possess, but I think that is a good thing here. Nevertheless, the beer lacks the complexity of the best examples of the style.

SHODriver, Apr 15, 2016
3.3/5  rDev -13.6%
look: 3.75 | smell: 3.75 | taste: 3 | feel: 3.25 | overall: 3.25

Pours a clear amber color. The head is a finger high and off-white in shade. While displaying a creamy texture, it doesn't retain well (nor does it have the rampant effervescence of other brett beers). Some webs of lacing stick to the glass, at least. It looks very good overall, but it doesn't look like a beer loaded with brett.

The brett isn't punching me in the face like it does with (non-fresh) Orval or the tragically missed Rayon Vert, but it's certainly coming across aromatically. I pick up notes of pineapple, apricot, funk, earth, and orange, while being backed by a nice crackery character. There's a gentle hoppiness to it, as well. Citrusy notes pop better the more it opens up. The quality of the aroma carves a nice little niche for itself, even if it isn't the most rustic one.

Its flavor opens up with a nice caramel character. After a while, a nice hop character shows up (bringing lots of stone fruit and a little citrus), while some funk closes things. I'm disappointed in the lack of depth: it's little more than what I just described, and I didn't describe much at all.

I like my brett beers to be bone dry; this isn't. It features a rounded, slightly thick feel instead. Carbonation seems intense initially, only for the full figure to smother it right before the swallow. Drinkability is reasonable regardless.

Personally, I think it could use more time in a bottle. It's drinking okay right now, but it's not impressing me. Right now, it's just a competent pale ale with a funky twist. The hop character right now isn't very interesting either, so my advice is to put this down in the cellar and hope it doesn't turn into a cardboard and oxidation bomb.

3.3/5: Needs more time...

Nice aromas with good balance and complexity of fruity/funky/spicy yeast, bready malt, and moderate earthy hop notes; with good strength. Taste of lemon, orange, apricot, melon, pineapple, pear, white grape, peppercorn, hay, straw, leather, wood, musty funk, cracker, toasted biscuit, herbal, grass, and yeast earthiness. Moderate herbal/yeast spiciness on the finish; with lingering notes of lemon, orange, apricot, melon, pineapple, pear, white grape, peppercorn, wood, cracker, toasted biscuit, and herbal/yeast earthiness on the finish for a good bit. Very nice robustness, complexity, and balance of fruity/funky/spicy yeast, bready malt, and moderate earthy hop flavors; with a great malt/spiciness balance and zero cloying flavors after the finish. Light-moderate dryness from yeast spiciness as well. Medium carbonation and body; with a very smooth, moderately bready/grainy, and lightly sticky balanced mouthfeel that is nice. Alcohol is very well hidden with minimal warming present after finish. Overall this is a very good dry hopped Brett wild ale. All around good robustness, complexity, and balance of fruity/funky/spicy yeast, bready malt, and moderate earthy hop flavors; and very smooth and easy to drink. A bit more malty than I initially expected; but was still very well balanced with some unique flavors from the Drie Brett strain. A very enjoyable offering.

neenerzig, Dec 18, 2015
3.77/5  rDev -1.3%
look: 4 | smell: 3.75 | taste: 3.75 | feel: 3.75 | overall: 3.75

At the ripe old age of twenty-two, Avery's anniversary ale steps into the wild side. With a host of characterful microflora offering its earth pungency, fruit and rustic old nuances, this year's birthday beer will make you want to pucker up with them.

Hiding within its strikingly bright golden hues and underneath its cottony froth expels the distinct fragrance of dried leathery citrus, green berry, pepper, wood and orchard fruits, along with light mustiness of cork, cellar and attic staleness. Grapefruit and grain frame the early palate while the vibrancy of orange peel, tropical fruit and nectarine bridge into the middle.

As the beer opens up, its firm crusted sweetness drops it savory upstart and favors the taste of straw and hay rather than the fresh baking bread and honey sweetness that it leaves behind. In its wake, the citrus peels grow in intensity and invites the taste of dried pineapple, nectarine, lemon and grape to develop an acute bitterness to close. Peppery and lightly tannic, its taste trends drying and vinous like riesling.

A mild soap flavor follows as the medium-bodied wild ale hangs onto a tepid breadiness, graininess and toasty sweetness to balance the rising acidity. Mild sourness strips the malt and highlights the earthen spices of peppercorn, sawdust and cumin in a short-lived but bitter aftertaste.
