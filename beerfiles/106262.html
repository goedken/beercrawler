Thanks to markwise for this crowler, nestled snugly among many Florida friends; I suspect it’s already in frost-bitten shock from the journey north. 32 oz. crowler sealed 1/19/16 split with my wife.

The pour is on the copper/amber end of the spectrum, with slightly lower carbonation than expected, which isn’t too surprising. The nose is top-notch: cedar closet, my grandma’s sweaters, potpourri, sweet honeyed tea, pepper. Nothing IPA in the entire bag of wax, but who cares when it smells this good?

Humidor IPA should really be relabeled Humidor Humidor. Following the trend spotted in the aroma, this heads straight for the wood – if you have a moth farm, please do not expose them to even a sealed can. Massive cedar lumbers all the way through the start, mid-palate, finish, & I’m sure, when I get up to piss in the middle of the night, I’ll still have splinters wedged in my molars. Other than that there’s a vast ocean of sweet malts, sweet honeycomb, faint bell of bitterness ringing (far, far off), & not much else. Luckily, I love sweet & dry, & that’s exactly how I’ll suck this glass.

I’ve loved the dark, malty Humidor beers in the series, but it just doesn’t work as well with an IPA, as it bludgeons the hops all the way into another beer. Luckily I like cedar & sticky sweet stuff, so it’s still good…but miles wide of the style marker.

"An unfiltered India Pale Ale brewed in the Cigar City of Tampa, Florida and aged on Spanish Cedar. Pairs well with spicy food and medium to robust cigars." Brewed in the style of an India Pale Ale aged on cedar spirals. Available as a year round in 12 oz. cans and on draft.

Poured from a 12 oz. bottle to a tulip glass. Served above 57º Fahrenheit.

(Appearance) Pours a banana cream white head of a creamy frothed consistency over a deep melon orange body. Carbonation is moderate and lively. Retention is very good, lacing is moderate and caked around the edges of the glass. 4.5

(Smell) Some wood aging is apparent by the nose, as a modest twiggy dryness pairs with more common malt characters of biscuit, light bread and a dash of caramel syrup. Hop notes are mellow, with mild grapefruit, ripe citrus, and fruit notes attempt to match the intensity of the cedar but sadly fall short. At the aroma, there isn't much differentiating this from a non-cedar-aged IPA. Potency is modest. 3.5

(Taste) The flavor is far more pronounced over the aroma. Slick sticky bread and grainy malts with a moderate syrupy sweetness are joined by a mellow citrus juice, slightly resinous and peppery hop profile with a moderate bitterness, slightly amplified in its intensity by the mellow spiciness and woodsy character of cedar, generating a dry finish. The Spanish Cedar comes through most in the finish, with a lingering of pale wood. 3.75

(Mouthfeel) Texture is smooth, slick, mildly grainy. Carbonation generates a soft frothiness for a lightly crisp mouthfeel. Body is medium+ for the style, medium+ overall. Balance is slightly bitter over sweet. Alcohol presence is light/medium and off characters are minimal. 3.75

(Overall) A unique one-time visitation for me to see the impact of spanish cedar on an India Pale Ale, although I can't far justify even the occasional return to this beer over superior beers like their Jai Alai, which have better hop presentation and flavor. Despite the addition of cedar, this is about as much a one-and-done experiment for me as it should be for the brewery. Unique and enjoyable, although I expect a wider deviation than usual on this beer. 3.75

There's many beer styles I never thought I'd try, but definitely didn't expect to cross paths with a cedar aged IPA! Thanks jdhowe! Poured out of a 12oz bottle into a tulip glass.

Amber orange with a ton of sediment that curls to the bottom, over time. A creamy, frothy white head rises to the top, and takes a while to dissipate, leaving behind good lacing. The foam seems to last forever. Viscous and highly presentable.

Cedar dankness and tobacco must are all up in this joint (no pun intended), with nice tropical and citrus notes following afterwards, albeit a bit hidden from the intense toasted woody notes. Spicy tobacco, black pepper, sage, light cannabis, and then some peach, grapefruit, and orange at the finish. Nevertheless, the IPA base is reaaally hidden here, almost to the base beer's disadvantage. The crush black pepper notes get increasingly stronger, and while it's fascinating and savory, it's slightly one-dimensional.

The flavor is much better, and while it's still very tobacco forward and pepper, some of the fruity notes from the malts and hops do shine through. Tropical overall, with pineapple, guava, and some papaya making its way towards the finish. Orange and grapefruit peel clean it at the end. Light caramel and toffee add a sweet backbone to the toasted cedar dankness, as well as the herbal, cannabis-like arching flavor. Contains a more toasted resinous flavor rather than any substantial hop flavors, which, again, makes it really interesting but not totally brilliant. Regardless, the feel of this beer is up to par on what I've grown to expect from Cigar City - creamy, full, crisp, and very strangely succulent.

I'm not quite sure if IPAs are the greatest contender for cedar aged beers, but this definitely worked overall for me - and paired terrifically with spicy Thai duck curry. This just evokes Florida, in certain respects - the cigar culture, the tropical fruits, and the breezy, refreshing walks by the beach that are briny and strangely savory. I could have done less without the massive black pepper bomb this brings to the table, but I'll gladly have another (especially fresh... By the seashore... Oh yeah!).

I like cedar-- for decks. I found cooking with it is too pungent. I prefer oak planks for grilling. I feel it doesn't work that well here either.
I've had several cigar city products to date but would be hard pressed to say any of them are even close to the top shelf breweries.
