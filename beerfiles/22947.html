The CANQuest (TM) is back up and running and I am happy to leave bottles in my rear view mirror! My maternal uncle is a geologist, so I dedicate this CANned beer to him.

From the CAN: "Tribally Pleasing"; "A true India Pale Ale"; "An amazing Western PA discovery, light in color and full of hop flavor, a very aromatic find."; "S[CAN] Your C[AN]!"; "Enjoy responsibly & Please recycle".

I just love the Crack! followed by an inverted Glug and I CANnot remember why I allowed myself to get buried in glass bottles. In this case, I got about two fingers of dense, rocky, light-tawny head with decent retention from the Glug. Nose had a grassiness that put me in mind of an EIPA, rather then an AIPA, along with a slight maltiness, reinforcing that thought. Color was Amber to Deep Amber (SRM = > 9, < 13) with NE-quality clarity. Great sticky lacing was beginning to appear. Mouthfeel was medium, at most. The taste was kind of odd, bitter but not full-on AIPA bitter. I would argue that between the soft mouthfeel and the muted hoppiness, it should be CANsidered as an EIPA. In that case, it really works. It would be at the upper end of the style, but more aligned than as an AIPA. The more that I thought of it as an EIPA, the more I liked it. Finish was semi-dry with a decent hoppiness, but still mellowed by its malt bill.

Aroma: Soft lemony and flowery hop aromas up front. Light caramel and biscuit notes in the background. Light yeasty character noted as the beer is agitated.

Appearance: Hazy dense amber pour with a moderate sudsy cream head that retains well.

Flavor: More lemony and slight evergreen notes assert themselves. There is a pleasant biscuit caramel base that supplies a firm counterpoint. Balance is to the earthy hop bitterness but the malt presence is definitive. Finish is clean and dry. Aftertaste is slightly bitter and lingers.

Mouthfeel: Medium bodied with medium carbonation. Slight hop derived astringency. No alcohol heat.

Overall impression: A malty American IPA. I like the hop character but the malt presence is decidedly firm and “East Coast” in nature. It’s well-crafted and enjoyable.

xnicknj, May 09, 2011
4/5  rDev +11.1%
look: 4 | smell: 4 | taste: 4 | feel: 4 | overall: 4

Paleo IPA has a hazy, early summer sunset-like golden-amber colour that seems to say 'let's make a memory'. Its big, bubbly white head disappears in a New York (err, make that Slippery Rock) minute but leaves an intricate pattern of lacing that, like any respectable abstract art, belongs somewhere in the Tate Modern.

If you thought American tourists were obnoxious, you should smell their hops! Paleo IPA - like any American IPA worth its salt- stinks like that guy you got stuck standing next to on the subway - an onion-like pungency derives from copious amounts of the little green cones and pellets. Part and parcel with it are scents of citrus and bitter, earthy resins.

If that sounds one sided it's because the beer is. But hey, ask Fatboy Slim, walk without rhythm and you won’t attract the worm. This is a beer that can evacuate the dance floor. Zesty, hoppy resins and citric hop oils are in no short supply, and there's enough biscuity malt backbone to keep the thing propped up. Good thing I have a whole growler!

North Country Brewing may blather on about the style's British roots, but this offering has far more in common with its fellow patriots than it does virtually anything I've had out of jolly old England. Frankly, most English would find this kind of brazenly bitter temperament abhorrent - it'd be the most offensive thing in their mouths second only to their teeth!

But that's exactly the reason why people like me love it. This sample didn't quite go to India, but it did travel a great distance thanks to a generous someone. And it wouldn't have held up if not for such audacious hops, the very same that lend tastes of grapefruit flesh, orange peel and lychee pit to the otherwise standard toasty, bready malt profile.

The brewpub was smart to make an unapologetic, full character IPA with no-holds-bar hopping. Customers who hate your product will not buy it, but let's face it: those who merely like your product will not buy it either. This is a beer not for the many, admittedly, but one well suited to those fiends who love the style for all its bold, bitter charms.
