neurobot01, Feb 11, 2016
4.21/5  rDev +7.9%
look: 3.5 | smell: 4.25 | taste: 4 | feel: 4.75 | overall: 4.5

750 ml capped bottle poured into a Bruery tulip.
10% ABV, "serve in a goblet at 50-55°F"

A - A few tiny chunks of sediment hang in suspension but this year-old wheatwine is pretty damn clear otherwise, though we'll see when we get to the last pour. Carbonation trickles through the beautiful dark honey amber brew to a fizzy and fleeting almond head that dots the glass in timid specks of lace.

S - The fleeting head gives the beer little aroma when it disappears leaving behind dry and dusty old wood, akin to grandpa's work bench in an unfinished basement with a touch of earthy hop bitterness but, give this wheatwine a vigorous swirl and this warming alcohol-rich beer opens up to a softer, smoother aroma of warm wheat bread, lightly toasted and spread generously with sweet clover honey!

T - There's very little of anything worth noting upon first sip aside from its immaculate smoothness for being 10%, though aged. A seemingly simplistic malt base hints at warm dinner rolls from the oven but leans more towards the smooth wheat presence with the slightest of nods to both warm alcohol and a delicate, lingering earthy hop American oak wood-like suggestion.

M - Quite puzzling is this beer's odd soda-like fizz upon the pour but its nearly flat feeling body that actually seems to be missing carbonation. Interestingly enough, it works perfectly with this wheatwine's decadent, medium body that, thanks to the heavy wheat bill, is super smooth and finishes with almost perfect cleanliness. It warms the throat and stomach ever-so-slightly leaving it at the high end of the ridiculously dangerously drinkable scale.

O - There are very few bottles that I ever sit on without first trying fresh but AleSmith's 2014 Decadence is one of them, simply out of necessity - I just haven't had the time/cared to make the time to sit down with it, as harsh as that sounds. So while I can't compare it to what it was fresh; something I personally preach when it comes to aging beer, I can say that right now, it's pretty friggin' good.

The fleeting head makes for an odd aroma at first but give this beer a swirl and its honey-smeared wheat bread aroma is quite inviting but doesn't quite follow through into the good-but-lacking flavor. The crowning achievement for this wheatwine, however, is the exceptional smoothness it exudes in every characteristic. There's a good chance my wife might shoot me the stink eye, but I might kill this bottle and be drunk on a Monday night before I even know it, it's that dangerously drinkable.

Poured from the bottle into a snifter glass.

Deep peach orange, really neat body color with a bit of grapefruit like edged appearance and less juice in appearance and thicker malt and body in the glass. Head is off tan to light in color, but dissolves pretty quickly to next to nothing. A small centrical ring hits the middle of the puck, no collar, no top, a nice middle column of carbonation makes its way. Head and staying power not much, but great body appearance really saves this.

Aroma is excellent. Peach and apricot aromas and a soft honey blossom almost floral quality. A good amount of sweetness but in control and inviting, but also hints at candy cane and dime store penny candy. Pretty inviting and very enjoyable.

Palate makes a much different mark. Lighter bodied but solid, with a just tinge of hop bitterness of woody pine on the finish. Chewy body not so much but the candy like flavors in the mid palate are really noticeable. Hints of peppermint, raspberry quick sugars, and light honey. Herbality kicks in nearly peppery but the sweetness really balances it out. Body has not held up, quite a bit wet unfortunately, and slightly flat feeling in the mouth.

Always nice to have a wheatwine once in awhile, even if it's this old.

Surprisingly clear copper brew. It appears hazy at first, but if you hold it up against a background, it's almost perfectly transparent - just dark red and active. Very nice light beige head, good retention, and fantastic lacing.

Big sweet aroma: honey and heather, white grapes, a touch of pepper and grass, lots of grains. Doesn't smell boozy. Sweetness fades a bit as it warms.

Flavor is just as sweet as the initial aroma, thickly so. Tons of grains, including very noticeable wheat. This is like chewing on bread soaked in honey. That is not a bad thing.

Thick, full mouthfeel; syrupy; sticky coating. The 10% is not particularly noticeable, although it does start to build a nice chest warming as you go.

This is a really interesting beer, and a great introduction to the style of Wheat Wine. It may be too sweet for some, but it never comes across as artificially sweetened.
