This has really surprised me on the upside.

500ml bottle poured into a nonic pint glass...

A - Cloudy copper body, with a thin frothy beige head.The head retention is rather weak.

S - The aroma is quite typical for an English bitter. Toffee and bread notes, some fruitiness (apples, plums, pears), and a pretty good earthy hoppiness.

T - Tastes like the countryside to me. Starts out with a good English-style mild herbal kind of hoppiness, followed by a solid malt base, with a sour dough texture and strong hints of toffee and even chocolate. Some apparent orchard fruit notes (apples, pears, plum) linger on into the finish, which is bready and slightly bitter.

M - Medium-bodied, with some lively carbonation. Kind of hefty for such a light beer, but very soft on the palate. Not too dry either. Feels nice.

D - The drinkability could be better. It is easy to drink, but I cannot see myself drinking a few pints in a row, it's just a little hefty for that in my opinion.

Overall, this is one of the better English bitters I've tried. Not entirely unique, but worth checking out. It is indeed a very TRADITIONAL English ale.

GodOfBeer, Apr 11, 2010
3.13/5  rDev -7.7%
look: 3.5 | smell: 3 | taste: 3 | feel: 3 | overall: 3.5

The beer has a rosy butterscotch hue that is rather visually stimulating; I feel at once revitalized just looking at it. Its complexion is sunny and replete with striking highlights. The carbonation is galvanized enough to be visible but not enough so as to form any kind of foam what so ever. Closer inspection also reveals scraps of spent yeast.

The aroma is prototypically English in its reserve and mildness, the dram of fruit preserve and trickle of toffee. It leaves the nose with little to do. A faint, ambiguous leafiness is noted as is a weak, watered down honey. Otherwise, however, this bouquet is too trite and too drab to incite any kind of invigoration or enthusiasm.

The flavour fares no better. The sweet toffee becomes slightly more emphasized and a trace of honeycomb accompanies the stock taste of biscuit malt. Fruit is again noted but only in the form of preserves. This beer is about as tasty as a cracker and about as stirring as a trip to the factory where such a thing is made.

It has about as much carbonation and bitterness as its name suggests. This being a "traditional" ale and all, I figured I'd drink at a more traditional - that is to say, ambient - temperature. In hindsight, the fridge probably would have done it no wrong. There was nowhere near enough malt used; it seems a product of the second (if not third) runnings.

CAMRA says this is "real ale" which doesn't mean that it's good ale - at least not when it's shipped over to this side of the Atlantic. If that organization really wants to promote a greater appreciation of traditional beers then they should lobby English brewers to stop exporting such numb, bastardized versions of their products. This Hog's Back beer may have won gold at the Great British Beer Festival, but this bottle tasted like hogwash.

Definitely an easy drinker, Hogs Back TEA makes a great session ale. It isn't outstanding with flavour to start but improves as the beer warms and your taste buds adjust. I'll buy it again if the price is right.

It has a nice clear copper colour with a foamy head that recedes with some clinging lace that makes it way down the glass.

The aroma is clean and mild. Sweet malty caramel and toffee aromas dominate with some citrus hop in the balance. The aroma improves as the beer warms.

The taste pulls through more too. It's a good idea to drink at the recommended temperature of 13 Celcius. That's when the caramel malt becomes more noticeable with buttery diacetyl and light earthy hops which finish with a slight citric bitterness in the finish. It goes down smooth and refreshing with next to no courseness.

Good stuff.
