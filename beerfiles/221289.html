Notes / Commercial Description:
Prairie is pleased to announce and celebrate the 3rd birthday of our most popular, well known, and voted most likely to succeed beer we produce, Prairie Bomb! Join our celebration for the beer that changed it all. We did everything but hang up streamers.

Whatâ€™s the buzz about anyway? Bomb! was a mad creation in response to the many complicated brews that required an interpreter to approach. The path to creating this ultimate beer that never planned on taking itself seriously led to many unexpected revelations. It contained almost every ingredient imaginable. It was difficult. It was delicious. It was a free spirit, complex but lending itself to endless possibilities. It became a much loved member of the family, and a representation of our brewing style and how it has evolved. In its original form Bomb! comes close to being a heady, strong dessert in itself. This led to the conclusion that we had exponential possibilities with flavor profiles and aging processes.

Because of this, Birthday Bomb! was conceived to celebrate three wonderful years of experimentation and fun loving flavors like the current incarnation with caramel sauce. Made in house by our brewers with nothing but black gloves and a propane tank. The rest is the complex mix of hops and malt with a healthy dose of the signature coffee and spices.

rolltide8425, Sunday at 09:33 PM
2.9/5  rDev -33.9%
look: 3.25 | smell: 3.25 | taste: 3 | feel: 2.75 | overall: 2.25

On-draught @ Mug's Alehouse in Brooklyn, fittingly on my birthday (eve). Cost was $10 USD per 10oz pour.

Head is a smooth tan with a dark complexion; lacks the shining golden glisten of the tippy top very best imperial stouts. Retention is poor but understandably so at this high an ABV - she lasts maybe 30 seconds, but coats the lip of the glass with thick even lacing as she recedes.

Body is an opaque dull black. Not the jet/ink black with an obsidian sheen you'd find in the best expressions of the style (to nitpick). But it's conventional.

Appears aptly carbonated. Far from unique or special in appearance, but generally inviting.

AROMA: I get the vanilla bean up front and the subtle chili pepper with its accompanying spice on the back end. Vanilla rings genuine, evoking actual vanilla bean (not the vanillin from bourbon/oak). Cacao nibs are hard to pick out from the specialty chocolate malt, but it does have a Hershey's syrup bend to it. Caramel sauce? Okay sure; I guess it's somewhere in that syrupy note. Coffee? Sorry, no; I find none. Dark malt sweetness is the glue holding this together.

Aromatically, none of the key constituent notes ring as vivid, distinct, or expressive as would be ideal, and I don't know if that's a sign of good balance or poor depth of flavour.

Aromatic intensity is merely average, and seems low for the style.

No overt hops, yeast, or (impressively) booze.

TASTE & TEXTURE: That Hershey's syrup note is way too dominant, or maybe I'm conflating that with the caramel sauce. Either way its prominence comes at the expense of the other more interesting notes, and it's syrupy chewy feel isn't stellar (drowning out any chili heat with slick smoothness). A soft powdery feel would have been a better play in terms of accentuating flavour.

The standout for me is the authentic vanilla bean, which - as in the aroma - opens the beer before getting drowned out by the loud Hershey's syrup and chocolate/dark malt sweetness. Coffee bitterness would help counter all that sweetness if only it were present, but none is overt. Roasted barley and/or charred sugars/burnt notes would also help, but no dice there either.

Cacao nibs are present I guess, but I'd be at a loss to recognize them in a blind tasting.

Even for a dessert stout, this bends too sweet. Unlike most Prairie Bomb! series beers, the 10oz pour seems a bit much for one sitting.

Some blueberry might be nice in this build, so long as we're discussing where it could be improved.

OVERALL: All in all, I'm a bit let down. Depth of flavour isn't great, nor is its balance. It's a sipper less out of alcohol presence than sweetness, and its heavy filling presence on the palate isn't justified by bold vivid flavours. At a buck an ounce from the brewery that's brought us so many tasty imperial stouts, that's disappointing. I wouldn't order this one again; I think the caramel inclusion was a mis-step. I'd sooner reach for an Ole Raspy, a Ten Fidy, or one of the other Bomb! variants, though price and expectations aside I do have to concede it's at least average, and as it approaches room temperature some chili heat starts to emerge in the late second act towards the finish. I'd serve this one unchilled.
