Finally getting around to reviewing this one from my notes after having it several times--these notes are from 4/10/16, drinking a bottle from the 2015 release on 10/31 (I think it was bottled a couple weeks prior to that. Poured out of a 12 oz bottle into an oversized brandy snifter.

Appearance (4.0) - Pretty standard so far, with a brown-to-black body that is pretty dark even when it's not all amassed within the glass. Carbonation appears low, needing a fairly aggressive pour to coax out a half finger of dark mocha colored head that dissipates to a ringlet and some smatterings of foam across the surface fairly quickly. This is in contrast to how I remember it five months ago, when pouring it built up a big two-finger head that had bubbles slowly expand to make the foam look like a cross-section of a chocolate souffle. Lacing is nonexistent, and there is a hint of faint legs when I tip the glass. Not terribly viscous looking.

Smell (4.25) - Off to a great start. There are notes of rich baker's chocolate and the bittersweetness that carries alongside a pungent infusion of cinnamon and spice. When I say spice, I'm thinking more of the baking spice kind--nutmeg, etc. You don't get that pepper smell that is so prevalent in chile beers, but I'm not surprised that the chocolate, cinnamon, nutmeg and coffee--all much more aromatic profiles--would overpower that. Speaking of the coffee, it too struggles to distinguish itself amidst the cacophony of other smells, which is fine, because I personally tend to prefer these "Mayan/Mexican/Aztec/etc" style stouts without the coffee. Honestly, I don't really remember the coffee being that much more prevalent when this was fresher.

Taste (4.5) - As the aroma intimated, the taste is a spice bomb, although now the habaneros make their presence known in a profound way that wasn't as present in the aroma. The first sip tweaks your tastebuds with that signature heat, but as you continue drinking, the heat impact becomes cumulative. Had I drank this faster, I've no doubt that my mouth would have been aflame. That said, the other ingredients do well to cut that heat and make it cooperate with the overall flavor profile rather than just scorching my mouth with napalm. The cinnamon does well alongside the habanero, with its own pseudo heat getting the impression of being amped up by the peppers. The sweetness of the chocolate comes through as well, balancing the spiciness, but alas the coffee and nutmeg are all but lost. So is any sense of char from the roasted malts. Here, cinnamon and heat are the lead actors, so if you don't like those components in your beer, this one may be a rough go. I will close by saying this--the barrel imparts a very, very subtle touch here. I'm not sure if it has to do with the powerful nature of the adjuncts use, but there is zero alcohol heat and really no astringency going on, to the point that I can't taste bourbon, picking up maybe the caramel echos of the barrel-aging, but no overt evidence that this was placed in a barrel.

Mouthfeel (4.0) - Medium-bodied with low carbonation and a feel that is increasingly hot as you work through the beer. Not as viscous as I would like a near10% abv stout to be, but surprisingly the slightly less chewy body holds up well to the smorgasbord of flavors contained therein.

Overall (4.5), this is a beer that I would describe as somewhat one-note, considering all the stuff that they put into it, but luckily, the dominant notes are flavors that I really enjoy. There is just enough chocolate to keep this beer from being destroyed by the intense spice, so it's one I've really enjoyed over the past seven months.

With the help of Kentucky bourbon barrels and a massive imperial stout, the fine brewers at Odd Side have taken the classic Mexican hot chocolate and made it substantially better! Cinnamon and spice steps aside to allow a whole lot of complexity to enter the mix.

Black as tar and with a deep onyx-like sheen, the beer's thick mocha-stained lather pushes its decadent scent off the rim. Bold dark chocolate, dark roast coffee, cinnamon and bourbon frame the nose along with a gentle drift of habanero. Its sweet, savory and sultry taste layers on the tongue like fudge, espresso and java milkshake.

With full saturation of the tastebuds complete and the warming of the ale releasing more of its complexity, the sweetness gradually lifts and the roasted grains subscribe to the taste chocolate covered espresso beans. A sprinkle of cinnamon and allspice offers a bit of holiday-like cheer ahead of the finish of spice and booze. Caramel, vanilla and faint coconut gives the beer the treatment of Mounds candy but with a burning pepper overlay.

Succulent and robust, the full-bodied ale is rich and creamy, seemingly milkshake-like in texture. Its not-so-subtle pepper head simmers from below and gives a little attitude to the bourbon-soaked, charred oak. Finishing with a calderon of capsaicin, the spicy cinnamon and cocoa powder trends deep and long in a warm afterglow.

jzeilinger, Mar 08, 2016
3.84/5  rDev -6.6%

12oz bottle from LambicPentameter (many thanks for the opportunity to try this!). Bottled 10/19/15. Served in a Barrel and Barley teku.

Pours oil black with a finger and a half tan head. The foam settles to some wispy haze and an undefined halo that leaves a few thin trails of lace.

Smells of coffee, chocolate, vanilla, caramel and a little bit if pepper and cinnamon spice. I don't smell any substantial barrel notes but this still smells great.

Cinnamon and mild habenero are immediately apparent in the flavor and have starring roles throughout. Those spices are joined by chocolate, caramel, coffee, mild roast, earthy nutmeg, vague oak and a slight tingle of alcohol. There is so little bourbon character that I'm almost wondering if this really did spend any significant than me in a barrel.

Creamy, smooth, medium full feel with relatively soft carbonation. Aftertaste of spice, mocha and a little roast.

Oddside has upped the ante on their Mayan Mocha stout again. Unfortunately for my tastes it seems like the cellarman/ barrel crew got impatient and bottled this before any meaningful barrel character or bourbon flavor could be imparted. I don't require a boozey burn and a bourbon blanket to cover the base beer but I sure would like some indication that some barrel aging actually occurred. That said, this is still a tasty beer, just nowhere near the complex flavors of other barrel aged "Mexican style" stouts I've tried.
