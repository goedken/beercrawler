500 ml bottle into tulip glass, bottled on 6/8/2016. Pours hazy golden orange color with a 2-3 finger dense and rocky off white head with fantastic retention, that reduces to a nice cap that lasts. Dense soapy lacing clings around the glass, with a good amount of streaming carbonation retaining the head. Fantastic appearance. Aromas of big peach, apricot, pineapple, tangerine, mango, melon, grapefruit, lemon zest, orange peel, peppercorn, light pine, wheat, cracker, herbal, floral, straw, grass, musty funk, and yeast earthiness. Damn nice aromas with great balance and complexity of fruity/spicy yeast, citrus/tropical hops, and moderate pale malt notes; with great strength. Taste of big peach, apricot, pineapple, tangerine, mango, melon, grapefruit, lemon zest, orange peel, peppercorn, light pine, wheat, cracker, herbal, floral, straw, grass, musty funk, and yeast earthiness. Moderate peppery yeast spiciness, pine/herbal bitterness, and light tartness on the finish; with lingering notes of peach, apricot, pineapple, tangerine, mango, melon, grapefruit, lemon/orange peel, peppercorn ,wheat, cracker, herbal, floral, grass, and yeast earthiness on the finish for a good bit. Damn nice complexity, robustness, and balance of fruity/spicy yeast, citrus/tropical hops, and moderate pale malt flavors; with a great malt/bitter/spiciness balance, and zero cloying flavors after the finish. Light-moderate dryness from bitter/spiciness, increasing through the glass. Medium-high carbonation and medium body; with a very smooth, moderately sticky, and creamy/grainy balanced mouthfeel that is nice. Alcohol is very well hidden with only a light warmth lingering after the finish. Overall this is an excellent Brett saison. All around great complexity, robustness, and balance of fruity/spicy yeast, citrus/tropical hops, and moderate pale malt flavors; very smooth, easy, and quite refreshing to drink for the ABV. A very enjoyable offering.

This summer is shaping up to be the summer of America as several brewers devise a sudsy taste branded with the patriotic namesake, or either temporarily changing their name to America for false pride, the brewers of Prairie use a combination of funk and fresh to reign in hot weather, camping trips and pool parties.

But pouring with a tarnish, burnt and turbid gold, the beer's unfiltered character leaves it looking murky albeit topped with a creamy whipped head. Its funk and fauna begins to show with the very first pass of the nose as dried citrus, herb and a dusty cellar-like funk carries the scent. Is taste is biscuit-like with mild steely caramels and the soft savory taste of bread crust.

As the flavors develop, its creamy and bready tastes and textures drift off and leave the hops and "wild" character to carry the balance afterwards. The bitter and sour interplay of white grapefruit, dried pineapple, under-ripened orange and green berries play a mildly astringent role in its piquant tang and bitter flavor. Highly oaken, the beer's finish is a mix of cork, balsa and cedar for a floral and fragrant closing character.

Medium-dry and with a creamy, moderate texture, there's a starchy taste and texture that provides the beer's malty-dry undertow and is lightly yogurt-like. Tangy, tart and refreshing, the beer's high wood character seems splinter-like with a high tannic rate that's perhaps a little too woodsy, especially in complement to the yeast character with its own floral notes of weathered cellar and slightly mushroomy wood rot.

TX-Badger, Nov 26, 2015
3.5/5  rDev -15%
look: 3 | smell: 3.5 | taste: 3.75 | feel: 3.25 | overall: 3.25

BOTTLE: Batch 1(2015). Brettanomyces conditioned. Unbranded black pry-off crown cap. Brown glass. Great label art. 1 pint 9.4 fl oz.

7% ABV.

Distributed by the Shelton Bastards. Reviewed live as a farmhouse ale per the label. Expectations are high given the brewery. Served cold into a tulip and allowed to come to temperature over the course of consumption.

No bubble show forms as it's poured.

HEAD: Occupies about 80% ofthe glass. Off-white colour. Somewhat thin, but adequately frothy, with a nice consistency. Complexion is decent; it's not shining or anything. Leaves uneven lacing on the sides of the glass as it recedes. Head retention is superb - about 8 minutes.

BODY: Fairly vibrant yellow-copper. Lacks a farmhouse ale's usual haziness; it's clearly filtered. Translucent, transparent, clear. No yeast can be seen within.

It seems really well-carbonated. Not a unique or special look for a farmhouse ale, nor a traditional one, but it's certainly inviting.

AROMA: Quite fruity, actually, with juicy notes of peach and apricot. Hopping is fruity and floral. The malt backbone is neutral, comprised of Belgian pale malts, 2 row, clean barley, and starch. Maybe some barnyardy straw & hay as well. Any malt sweetness is nicely checked by a dry brettanomyces yeast presence, which lends the beer biscuity aromatics - but oddly isn't very funkiness-forward. For a brettanomyces farmhouse ale, this is surprisingly un-funky. But that might be to its benefit, as it lets the fruity notes shine.

The spicey aromatics aren't as complex as they'd be in, say, a Fantome saison, but there's adequate intricacy...clove and white pepper are the most prominent spices. I don't find any oak barrel or anything.

Overall, this aroma suggests a fruit-forward farmhouse ale with good balance and above average complexity considering its country of origin. Aromatic intensity is moderate.

TASTE: Fruit forward indeed, but with a hoppy bitter edge on the back end. Juicy notes of fresh orchard peaches and apricots mix in with hoppy notes of tangerine citrus and maybe even some new world hop mango character. The usual starchy Belgian pale malt body provides a nice backdrop for the fruity notes, with clean 2-row and barley filling out the body neatly. Those hoping for a funkbomb will be disappointed, as the brettanomyces here lends only biscuity notes and a very tame funkiness if any. Hints of spice - clove, white pepper.

Well-balanced for the most part, though that pesky hop bitterness definitely isn't to style. Well built, though cohesive as it is, it never truly tastes like a gestalt beer. With some tinkering it might get there. It's not the most intricate farmhouse ale out there (especially in terms of the spice and yeast profiles), but it's definitely on the better side for an American attempt at the style. Once again, Prairie has shown they're one of few American breweries making a sincere effort at brewing this style right.

TEXTURE: The brettanomyces has dried out the mouthfeel, and the starchiness of the beer gives it a subtle coarseness. The beer never drags on the palate, and I'd still call it refreshing. That subtle dryness and coarseness is to style, helping to coax a bit more depth of flavour out of the subtle spices. Light to medium-bodied, with style-appropriate heft and thickness. Overall presence on the palate is quite good. Good carbonation.

There's a nice harmony of texture and taste, but this mouthfeel - while well executed - fails to elevate the beer as a whole. Additionally, alcohol is evident on the backend, where it joins with the fruit to boozy effect.

OVERALL: Drinkability is better than what you might expect for a 7% ABV beer, but traditional beers in the style can be effortessly consumed. More of a delicate, fragile mouthfeel would better suit this beer, and taming that hop bitterness would definitely be a good call. It's not one of Prairie's finest efforts, and while I dig the surprisingly fruity flavour profile, I'd definitely sooner reach for a traditional Belgian farmhouse ale over this - especially since they're practically at the same price point. Well worth trying once, but not a beer I'd buy again in spite of its quality.

Will appeal to those discerning drinkers who are after a farmhouse ale with a tropical fruit bend instigated by new world hops.

High B- (3.50)
