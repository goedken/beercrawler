TerryW, Mar 14, 2015
3.3/5  rDev +4.1%
look: 3.25 | smell: 3.25 | taste: 3.5 | feel: 2.75 | overall: 3.25

341 ml bottle poured into a Red Racer pint glass (sacrilege!). Like other brews in this series, a darker than expected orange amber with a finger of fluffy off-white foam.

Aroma is not bad, decidedly tea-like and botanical. Little in the way of citrus but some floral notes present. An off-note that suggests insipidly sweet stale caramel malts.

Others are not tasting tangerine in this. I am, but its a metallic, rather ersatz spin on said fruit. More tea-like notes. A rather nice botanical bitterness but nothing too showy or flamboyant. The citrus does build itself up ... A little. Some canned peach notes and a less than welcome apple flavor. Mouthfeel is notably thick for the style (and based on one's expectations of a mass-produced pale ale). Almost overly so. Hops are doing just enough to hold back a cloying sweetness. Carbonation is low key. Finish remains overly sweet and malty, although some of the tea notes do persist.

As was the case for the Galaxy single hop ale, this defied my expectations, both for good and for ill. Overly malty, insufficiently hoppy, but far superior to this brewery's bland flagship "IPA". There are certainly better vehicles for the Saphir hop, which I concur is an odd but still interesting choice. Would people really be wowed if these guys did Citra?

Bunman3, Feb 22, 2015
3.16/5  rDev -0.3%
look: 3.5 | smell: 3 | taste: 3.25 | feel: 3.25 | overall: 3

Every beer in the Alexander Keith's Single Hop Series so far has looked malty and Saphir is no different; caramelly amber in colour, this has a sunset-like highlights and a light-pressed haze that lends visual thickness. The presence of a head, however feeble and waning it may be, is an encouraging sign given this brewer's track-record of thinning their beers with adjuncts.

As thrilled as I am that Keith's has opted to release single-hop beers, their choice of hop varieties has been somewhat perplexing. Cascade, I get. Galaxy, I love - though let's be honest, it's not exactly a household name. Hallertauer? Odd selection but ok. But Saphir? Really? It's not extremely common in pale ales or IPAs (which, again to be fair, there's no explicit claim this is an IPA).

Plus you'd think a company that operates on mass-appeal and advertising would naturally gravitate towards the distinctly citrusy and tropical varieties. While Saphir is said to provide sweet citrus (tangerine according to the brewery) aromatics there's no evidence of that here. Green, herbal plants maybe, but certainly not fruit. Frankly, I find the smell off-putting.

While I hesitate to go on record saying that Alexander Keith's has used too much malt, there's definitely more sweet caramel and dried fruit confection taste than there should be if the idea is to showcase hops (which are, I remind you, inherently bitter). The few, simple flavours I get on the palate are malt-derived. It's only a matter of time before the mouthfeel becomes cloying.

To my own surprise (and somewhat shame) I don't think anyone in the craft community has been more defensive of Keith's Single Hop Series than me - my ratings may be among the highest. But even I can't defend Saphir Hop - it's a pathetic reversion to the insipid, lowest-common-denominator ales this brewer is notorious for. My initial optimism has gradually (and now greatly) evaporated...

Appearance: Poured with two fingers of head that produced maginal lacing but didn't retain as much as I would have liked. The body boast steady carbonation on a deep clear orange with red highlights.

Smell: I'm noticing a dried tea scent blended into a fair aggressive dry hop note. Small notes of orange citrus are in the background. Warming seems to bring out the hops more and more. I think it will be too hoppy for my liking, but given the name I can't really complain I guess.

Taste: Mild malt with a slight tea like note turns to a very dry stale bread and hop hit that ends with a mandarin orange twang. Too hoppy for an APA, and likely not hoppy enough for most people to consider it to be a proper IPA.

Mouthfeel: Well at least the carbonation seems to be more creamy then aggressive and tries to reign in the hops unsuccessfully. That oily hop taste lingers in my mouth in an unpleasant fashion. At least there is a slight tangerine presence here. Transitioning needs work and it goes from mild, to aggressive and back to mild very abruptly.

Drinkability: Medium bodied, and a little bit gassy but it's not that bad. Despite the dry taste, the dry notes don't linger, so it sorta does recover a bit after the taste. It doesn't settle down well enough in my stomach to have it break even here.

Final Thoughts: All good things I suppose, this to me is the weakest of the Keith's hop series. So weak, that I would rank regular Keith's as superior. That being said though, I'm far from a hop head. I very much enjoyed the Galaxy and Hallertauer hops more then this one. Pass, unless your an IPA drinker and you want to try something milder.

wordemupg, Oct 20, 2014
3.46/5  rDev +9.1%
look: 3.5 | smell: 3.75 | taste: 3.5 | feel: 3.5 | overall: 3

341ml twist-off bottle - nice to see more of these exotic and esoteric hops being showcased by Alexander Keith, of all brewers.

This beer pours a clear, medium copper amber hue, with two fingers of puffy, tightly foamy, and mildly bubbly ecru head, which leaves some splotchy snow rime lace around the glass as things eventually sink away.

It smells of citrus-oil infused, oversteeped herbal tea, bitter florals, grainy pale malt, and a further leafy mustiness. The taste is rather floral, in both its perfumed and bitter qualities, the tea leaves having gone sour, with a pleasantly gritty, grainy, and crackery maltiness prevailing, while the greens, in the form of fresh generic herbs, tree buds, and underripe orange peel, round out things well enough.

The bubbles are fairly involved, what with their swirling frothiness, the body medium-light in weight, and plainly smooth, a slight edginess arising from the overwrought floral character. It finishes rather dry, the herbal, tea-like, and floral notes ruling the roost.

An interesting conceit - use a thus far (to me, at least) unknown hop in the newest instance of your single-hop series, and see what happens. Mostly, it's a disjointed, bitter - and not in the particularly best of ways - affair. Ok, I guess, but not nearly balanced enough to make me want to procure another round of this.

biboergosum, Oct 17, 2014
3.45/5  rDev +8.8%
look: 3.5 | smell: 3.5 | taste: 3.25 | feel: 3.5 | overall: 3.75

473 mL can picked up at TBS; packaged Aug 28 2014 and served slightly chilled. Can't say that I've had too many beers that have used this hop, at least that I know of.

Pours a bright, effervescent orange-amber, topped at first with just under two fingers of frothy, white head. The aroma is fairly inviting, with the malt bill being mostly analogous to those of the other beers in this Single Hop series - some grainy sweetness and caramel, and not much else. The hops contribute an interesting mixture of citrus and tea leaves, giving this one sort of an Earl Grey/bergamot orange vibe.

An approachable pale ale; not bad for a macro. As alluded to in the aroma, the malt bill is a little weak and not interesting at all, with grainy pale malts and a faint caramel sweetness struggling to be tasted amidst the Saphir hops, which are the obvious focus of this beer's flavour. Their flavours are restrained, including some orange citrus, and finishing on a leafy, tea-like note. Citrusy aftertaste, with a slight bitterness that fades immediately. Lightish-medium in body, with moderate carbonation levels and a smooth mouthfeel; something shared by all of the beers in their single hop series. This is theoretically sessionable, but I doubt I'd want to drink it all night.

Final Grade: 3.45, a B-. It has been roughly one year since Keith's released their last offering in this series of crafty ales, so they have had plenty of time to tweak and improve the recipe. While their Saphir Hop Ale is certainly well-brewed and approachable, it's still kind of dull and forgettable. Not a bad beer at all, and much like the Galaxy and Hallertauer hop ales, I will probably be grabbing more cans here and there when shopping at TBS in the future. A commendable effort, but not significantly better (or worse) than the other beers in this line-up.
