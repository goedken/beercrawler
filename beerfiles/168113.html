look: 3.75 | smell: 3.75 | taste: 3.75 | feel: 3.75 | overall: 3.75

473ml can, a bit of a surprising find of late on my local bottleshop's shelves.

This beer pours a clear, bright medium copper amber hue, with three fingers of puffy, loosely foamy, and bubbly off-white head, which leaves some hanging coral atoll lace around the glass as it gradually recedes.

It smells of grainy, bready pale malt, a touch of caramel/toffee sweetness, warm lemon juice, white pepper, and further leafy, earthy, and mildly weedy green notes. The taste is more of the same bready, pastry-adjacent malt from the nose, with a lemon pie fruitiness, some fading table top peppercorn spiciness, a bit of separate, yet indistinct citrus acridity, and leafy, weedy forest flora droppings.

The bubbles are tamely rendered, mostly manifesting in a genial frothiness, the body on the light side of middleweight, and smooth enough, the varied citrus components tripping things up just a tad. It finishes on the sweet side, as the malt barely tremors, the fruitiness of the lemon/citrus steps forth, and the proclaimed pepper essence wafts off into the ether.

Probably the best iteration of this single-hopped, weirdly non-IPA line from this brewery that I've yet encountered. The kind of exotic Slovenian hop varietal (put it in 'H'!!) does well for itself here, delivering the lemon and pepper (but not lemon pepper) goods. Worthy of giving a try, especially for the Big Boy Brewer low, low price of sub-3 bucks per tall boy can.

Amber. Brilliant clarity. Two fingers of off-white head that dissipates quickly. No lace. Subtle aroma shows toast, lemon zest, and bay leaf notes. It's a pleasant aroma, but it's just a bit too quiet. Base flavour of toast with lemon, herbaceous, and delicate peppery spice notes. Lemon lingers into the aftertaste. Light-bodied with medium carbonation. I like that this macro brewer has toned down the fizziness for these single hop beers.

The Alexander Keiths Hop Series ales are by no means great. They could be bolder in aroma and more flavourful. They could be a little more substantial in the mouth. But considering where these are coming from, a brewery whose flagship beer named India Pale Ale is truly a (in the pejorative sense) fizzy yellow beer in all but name? Well, at the very least you can clearly discern the character of the hop used in each beer in the series. Celeia is clearly discernible from Galaxy, for example. When looked at in that way, these beers are clearly successful in fulfilling the brewers intention. And even if they aren't great, they don't taste bad either.

mintjellie, May 08, 2015
3.66/5  rDev +5.8%
look: 3.5 | smell: 3.75 | taste: 3.5 | feel: 3.5 | overall: 4

Tallboy picked up at TBS; packaged Mar 26 2015 and served well-chilled.

Pours a clear orange-amber colour, generating one inch of white froth at the surface. It seeps away over the next couple of minutes, leaving behind a modest, foamy collar and a few splotches of lace. If you've seen one of these Keith's Hop Ales poured into a glass, you've seen 'em all. Along the same lines - the nose suggests the presence of a similar malt bill as you'll find in its sister ales, consisting of grainy barley and some caramel sweetness. The featured hop offers up some notes of citrusy lemon and a bit of leafiness... there may also be a bit of a peppery spice there, or that might just be the power of suggestion at work.

This is actually a pretty tasty pale ale - likely my favourite so far in this single hop series. As usual, some grainy pale malts and weak caramelized sugar sweetness round out the background, but the Celeia hops put on a better show than most of the other strains they've used so far. Lemony citrus pith is the biggest feature, with additional leafy, florally bitter undertones, some orangepeel, and a mild peppery bite at the end of the sip. Light-medium in body, with assertive carbonation that results in a crisp, lively mouthfeel; works nicely with the pepper. Quite easy to drink, and respectably flavourful for a macro.

Final Grade: 3.66, a B grade. In all honesty, Keith's Celeia Hop Ale is not particularly remarkable, but it's still one of the best products ever released by this brewery. As a relatively regular consumer of these single hop ales - mostly because they're the only halfway-decent ale tallboys that TBS keeps up front in their singles cooler - I will likely continue purchasing this one in the future (but it will be at the expense of one of the older varieties that I like less). I admire the effort that Keith's puts into these beers, but it'd be nice to see them try something a little different with the malt backbone next time.
