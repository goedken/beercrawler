Canned 10/24/16. Consumed 11/10/16.

A: Beer comes in a rich amber hue with medium-minus haze and about a finger of fluffy white head that sticks around for a bit. A bit of lacing left on the glass with alcohol legs for sure.

S: Very tropical and herbal, with big notes of cantaloupe, papaya, and peach with some mango thrown in combined with dank green tea and a hint of grassy pine. The earthy, herbaceousness aspect of the nose perfectly balances the sweetness from the fruit aromas to offer a supremely appealing nose.

T: Follows the nose... Maybe a bit more fruit-forward, but definitely a balance between the earthy, herbal and the tropical aspects of the hops. Up front, I get tons of ripe papaya, passionfruit, and mango, along with some strawberry and blueberry as well. Maybe even some more exotic, candied fruits (guava?). Moves into a soft, mild, white-bread-y malt and, on the finish, a piney, herbal dankness that persists with some mild residual fruitiness.

M: Really really awesome feel with tremendous balance, especially as it warms. There's some sweetness, but only just enough; for an 8% beer, this one drinks a percent or two below its ABV. It's smooth, clean, and even somewhat dry while still retaining a juiciness on the initial sip. It has a medium-plus weight on the tongue with that signature Grimm softness on the carbonation. Only thing holding this one back from a 4.75 is a slightly unpleasant, mild sting of booze on the swallow. Without that (and with a bit more weight/creaminess), this would be a perfect feel.

O: Definitely one of the best Grimm DIPAs I've had... Though definitely a step down from Afterimage, IMO. While I didn't have the opportunity to try this one super fresh (as I did with Afterimage), I found that I preferred the flavor and aroma profile on Afterimage vs. Tesseract. While Tesseract is drier and more herb-/tea-forward, I actually enjoyed the overwhelming punch of sweet, exotic fruit offered by Afterimage. In terms of mouthfeel, Tesseract is on par with the best of Grimm, delivering intense flavors with incredible softness and a damn near perfect weight on the palate to balance drinkability with substance. Overall, Tesseract is a great DIPA, but not my favorite from the brewery.

$8/8oz on-draught in Manhattan.

Head dies within a couple minutes, leaving uneven lacing. Body is a clear pale copper of average vibrance.

Aroma brings oniony hops, floral hops, and hop resins. No overt fruit inasmuch as I can discern, nor any oily notes. Pale malts keep it balanced. Aroma is of average strength, suggesting a balanced beer with a classic possibly Simcoe-dominant hop profile.

Taste is disappointingly brief in terms of flavour duration, with a simplistic unlayered flavour profile emphatic of blink-and-you'll-miss-it oniony notes, floral notes, and vague resins. No new world tropical hop nonsense here, but also no classic citrus or oily/piney hops.

Hop profile is balanced by pale malts but is generally underwhelming, especially for a beer (erroneously) marketed as an imperial IPA.

Depth of flavour is okay; it lacks pungent hop punch. Flavour intensity is average.

TEXTURE: Not as oily as would be ideal, but it has a subtle coarseness that kind of works for it, accentuating the Simcoe family oniony flavour. Wet yet unrefreshing, with a medium body.

OVERALL: An IPA pushed as an Imperial IPA, Tesseract is a competent beer that is somehow currently in the Top 250 in spite of being anything but dazzling. I love that they didn't go for the tropical fruit flavour that seems to be obnoxiously overdone in (I)IPAs these days, but as balanced as it is it leaves a lot to be desired in terms of intricacy and depth of flavour.

Straight pour from a 16oz can to an oversized wineglass (cmon, lets be real hereits my favorite piece of Jester King stemware). This is 12 days old, according to a canning date on the underside of the can (CANNED ON 10/24/16 09:16:06). This can was purchased two days ago, and its been in the fridge ever since.

Appearance (4.0): Two and a half fingers of ecru-colored foam rise off of the pour, capping a glowing, quite hazy but not cloudy, orange honey-colored body. Sure fits the style, and beautifully (for my taste). The head takes its sweet time dying down, leaving surprisingly little lacing. But, lots of suds cling to the side of the glass. Its very attractive, overall, but lacks the crazy lacing that I love in a good (D)IPA.

Smell (4.5): A veritable salad of rich, smooth, sweet, and not too acidic fruits come out first: peach, cantaloupe, papaya, champagne mango, and just a touch of ripe pineapple. Its followed by a light grassiness, but quickly outshone by a mid-level note of passionfruit that materializes after the fact. Maybe it could use a little more variety, but frankly, there are no complaints here. Actually, as the beer opens up, it turns increasingly toward the kind of funky, almost dirty passionfruit aroma that Im such a sucker for. Ugh.

Taste (4.25): Holy mackerel, this is excellent stuff. A touch of yellow grapefruit flesh bitterness comes along first, but it doesnt do much to offset the mixture of creamy papaya, raw honey, and overripe pineapple that combine for a rich, smooth body. That bitterness, though, takes on a grassy edge as the beer unfolds, offering a better counterpoint, especially as a quiet, slightly spicy heat adds an understory to this hoppy forest as the beer opens up. Still, that grassy, increasingly resinous bitterness grows in prominence, taking over more of the palate as the beer warms up a bit and continues to open. Some flavors kind of morph into a light bit of limeade and canned mandarin orange as the drink goes on. Malt? Its got a slightly chewy base, giving the impression of sugar cookies with light brown sugar icing, but the slightly spicy heat from the 8% ABV gives it a rye-like feel. But still, lets be honest. This beer is about hops.

Mouthfeel (4.5): This beer drinks almost exactly like its size: the body is smooth and creamy, full but not heavy, definitely beyond the point of medium-weight but not burdensome by any means. The carbonation is smooth, fine, and subdued at first. It moves forward, though, opening up into an evenly distributed, slightly spicy tingle that attaches itself to most surfaces just before it washes out. In spite of its fullness, the hops and heat allow Tesseract to finish somewhat (but not excessively) on the dry side.

Overall (4.25): To be honest, I was a bit surprised by this beer. I was expecting something hazy, kind of raw, and dynamic. While its got a big hop character and beautiful structure, its not the lightning-in-a-can facemelter I had anticipated. Im mostly surprised by how smooth and mellow the beer is, to be honest: its not the sort of bombastic, raw, cloudy NE-type (D)IPA they make over at Bissell Bros., Tree House, Tired Hands, or the Alchemist. But, Im not complaining. This beer is smooth, beautifully constructed, balanced, and still extremely satisfying.

My biggest gripe about this beer was how hard it was to find and how much it cost - another go at this would be great since this was rated so high and was so complex that it needed a second go-around.
