jdhilt, Dec 08, 2006
3.41/5  rDev +4.3%
look: 4 | smell: 3 | taste: 3.5 | feel: 3.5 | overall: 3.5

Dried apricot orange with butterscotch borders (there may be a suggestion of red if I squint and use a little imagination). The surface of the orangish ecru cap isn't exactly rocky, but it is humped and bumped and pitted and pocked. Multiple lace bridges connect head and glass with a good bit sticking in the end. This is an attractive red.

For a beer that boasts of a hop bill consisting of Chinook, Liberty, Willamette, Tettnanger and Fuggles (dry-hopped) and a brewery website that uses the words 'aggressively hopped', Red Thistle has a supremely disappointing nose. Were all those hops really necessary when your beer doesn't smell the least bit hoppy? By the same token, I can't appreciate much of the crystal or chocolate malt either.

This beer just has to be old. With no dating though, it's impossible to know for sure. It doesn't taste stale in the least, but there's such a deficit of flavor that I'm not sure what else could possibly be responsible for bold, flavorful ingredients like these being such a no-show on the palate. Will warming bring out any hidden charms? My Magic 8-ball says... doubtful.

Actually, the middle four ounces have some amount of flavor, while the top four had virtually none. At this rate, I might be less than disappointed by the bottom of the bottle. The beer is relatively well-balanced with caramel malt getting edged out--increasingly so--by bitter, spicy, citric hops. Chinook do not like to tied down and are doing their best to give me something to enjoy.

It's too little, too late though. Even though some degree of resiny hoppiness comes on in the end, the beer just isn't well-constructed enough for me to even think about drinking it again or recommending it to others. Not too many red ales would benefit by being consumed at room temperature, but this one almost requires it.

Red ales have a pretty wide range of acceptability when it comes to body/mouthfeel and Red Thistle falls well within those boundaries. Actually, it's probably on the full side of medium for the style and has enough carbonation to get the job done. I was a little worried after the barely perceptible 'ssst' upon top popping, but I needn't have been.

Golden Valley had the right idea with this beer, it's just that their grand vision didn't come off quite as planned (of course if I'm right and this bottle is a bit long in the tooth, I apologize). Oregonians would be much better off by skipping Red Thistle and drinking the outstanding reds from Full Sail or Deschutes.

2.19/5  rDev -33%
look: 3.5 | smell: 2 | taste: 2 | feel: 3 | overall: 2

I picked up two beers from Golden Valley while I was at the Seal Rock Grocery: Tannen Bomb and Red Thistle. I had never actually heard of this brewery, and (like most BAs) I'm always interested in trying new things when I'm out and about on vacation. Sadly, these two beers made me wish I'd just grabbed two more Terminal Gravity IPAs, as these both were underwhelming and actually ended up in the drain, rather than my belly.

Appearance: By far the best feature of the beer. Not a deep red, but very solid amber. Poured with minimal head, and what was there left quickly.

Smell: Damn. The cold yeast smell I was dreading. Cold in that the malts really don't have any presence in the nose at all, and yeast as that's all I smell in the finish. No hops whatsoever.

Taste: Well, the malts that I was looking for in the nose were here in the taste, they were just unremarkable. What makes this beer unpleasant for me is the yeasty finish. It almost has a lager yeast quality to it, and I simply don't enjoy it at all.

Mouthfeel: Doesn't have the great mouth presence of a Fat Tire (as much as I may dislike the beer itself, it really is well-crafted) or a Mac & Jack's, but it's ok.

Drinkability: For me, not at all. I won't mark it down as awful, because it wasn't. I just simply didn't like this beer at all. Red is a style I'm very familiar with, and enjoy quite a bit, and this one was way below average in almost all the criteria.

Having recently tried this in a bottle and liking it, I was pleased to see it on tap at a local restaurant. Pint Please!

A nice solidly amber beer arrived shortly. It was a rich hue around the edges with a nice dark core. The foam filled the last centimeter of the pint, nice pour! The beer was a little bit cool, so the aromas were subdued. sweet malt and a crisp hop aroma were present, though tough to pick up at first.

As it slowly warmed, the taste went from good to better. A nice warm and rich red, the maltiness was very pleasing. It had less of an "abbey" flavor to it on draft, and was probably better for it.
